Setup & Knobology

Always confirm the patient and indication. Use a high‐level disinfection cover for the probe and apply gel.

Transducers

  • Curvilinear (2–5 MHz) – Best for deep structures: eFAST, abdominal aorta.
  • Phased array (1–5 MHz) – Small footprint for cardiac windows and between ribs.
  • Linear (5–13 MHz) – High resolution for vascular access and superficial lung pleura.

Orientation & image optimisation

  • Indicator orientation: By convention the probe indicator (notch) is pointed towards the patient’s head (longitudinal views) or patient’s right side (transverse views). Always match the indicator to the on‑screen marker.
  • Depth: Adjust so your target fills about two‑thirds of the screen:contentReference[oaicite:14]{index=14}.
  • Gain: Increase until structures are seen clearly but without saturation; decrease if the image is too bright.
  • Movement: Slide (translate), tilt/angle, rock (heel‑toe), and rotate the probe to find the best view.

eFAST (Extended Focused Assessment with Sonography for Trauma)

The eFAST exam screens for free fluid in the abdomen, thorax and pericardium and for pneumothorax. Start with the patient supine and a curvilinear or phased array probe.

Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ)

  • Place the probe in the mid‑axillary line at the 8th–11th intercostal space with the indicator cranial:contentReference[oaicite:15]{index=15}.
  • Identify the diaphragm, liver and kidney; Morison’s pouch sits between the liver and kidney. Sweep cranially and caudally to search for anechoic free fluid.
  • Also examine the lower chest for pleural effusion and lung sliding at the costophrenic angle.

Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ)

  • Probe indicator cranial; place posteriorly at the posterior axillary line due to the stomach’s position:contentReference[oaicite:16]{index=16}.
  • Visualise spleen, diaphragm and left kidney; look at perisplenic recess and splenorenal recess for free fluid.
  • Sweep inferiorly along the spleen tip where fluid collects earliest.

Pelvic View

  • Place the probe just above the pubic symphysis; start transverse with the indicator to patient’s right then rotate to sagittal:contentReference[oaicite:17]{index=17}.
  • Use the bladder as an acoustic window; check the rectovesical pouch in males or pouch of Douglas in females for free fluid.
  • Sweep laterally and tilt to interrogate the pelvis fully.

Cardiac View (Subxiphoid)

  • Use a phased‑array probe; place inferior to the xiphoid with the indicator pointing towards the patient’s right:contentReference[oaicite:18]{index=18}.
  • Use the liver as an acoustic window; fan the probe to visualise all four chambers.
  • Identify pericardial effusion (anechoic fluid between the epicardium and pericardium) and assess gross contractility.

Lung Views (Pneumothorax)

  • Place the linear or phased array probe in the 2nd–4th intercostal spaces mid‑clavicular or mid‑axillary line; indicator cephalad:contentReference[oaicite:19]{index=19}.
  • Normal lung shows the pleural line with shimmering “lung sliding”; absence of sliding and B‑lines suggests pneumothorax.
  • M‑mode seashore sign (normal) versus barcode/stratosphere sign (pneumothorax) can help confirm findings:contentReference[oaicite:20]{index=20}.

Interpretation and sign‑off

Detecting free fluid in Morison’s pouch, perisplenic and pelvic pouches has high specificity for intra‑abdominal bleeding:contentReference[oaicite:21]{index=21}. In trauma patients, document which views were obtained and whether sliding was seen. The RCEM curriculum suggests performing and reflecting on roughly 25 eFAST/FAFF scans to achieve competence:contentReference[oaicite:22]{index=22}.

Abdominal Aorta (AAA)

The goal is to screen for abdominal aortic aneurysm or rupture. Use a curvilinear probe and scan longitudinally and transversely.

Patient positioning

Position supine with knees flexed; apply generous gel to reduce bowel gas:contentReference[oaicite:23]{index=23}.

Scanning technique

  • Start in the epigastrium in transverse orientation; identify the aorta just anterior to the vertebral body. Sweep inferiorly to the bifurcation while keeping the aorta centred:contentReference[oaicite:24]{index=24}.
  • Measure outer‑wall to outer‑wall diameters at proximal, mid and distal segments; a diameter ≥3 cm suggests an aneurysm:contentReference[oaicite:25]{index=25}.
  • Rotate the probe to obtain a longitudinal view and confirm the aneurysm and evaluate for dissection or thrombus.
  • Document the maximum diameter, presence of mural thrombus and any retroperitoneal fluid.

The RCEM curriculum suggests ~25 AAA scans with reflections for sign‑off:contentReference[oaicite:26]{index=26}.

Lung Ultrasound

Lung PoCUS can diagnose pneumothorax, pulmonary oedema, consolidation, pleural effusion and interstitial syndrome. Use a linear probe for pleural line details or curvilinear for deeper pathology.

Six‑point scan (R1/L1–R3/L3)

  • Position the patient supine or semi‑recumbent; raise arms to expose the chest:contentReference[oaicite:27]{index=27}.
  • Scan each hemithorax at three points: anterior (R1/L1 at mid‑clavicular), lateral (R2/L2 at anterior axillary), and posterior (R3/L3 at posterior axillary or “PLAPS” point):contentReference[oaicite:28]{index=28}.
  • At each point, observe lung sliding and search for A‑lines (horizontal reverberations), B‑lines (vertical comet tails), consolidations or pleural effusion.

Key findings

  • Pneumothorax: Absent sliding and B‑lines; M‑mode shows barcode sign; lung point may be visible.
  • Pulmonary oedema: ≥3 B‑lines in multiple zones indicates interstitial syndrome.
  • Consolidation: Tissue‑like echotexture with dynamic air bronchograms.
  • Pleural effusion: Anechoic or hypoechoic fluid collection above the diaphragm; use low‑frequency probe at posterolateral points.

The curriculum suggests performing ~25 lung ultrasounds with reflections for competence:contentReference[oaicite:29]{index=29}.

Focused Cardiac Ultrasound (Echo in Life Support)

This is not a comprehensive echocardiogram but a focused assessment during resuscitation to answer life‑threatening questions.

Views

  • Subxiphoid (Subcostal): Use a phased‑array probe with indicator to patient’s right. Depth ~20 cm. Visualise all four chambers. Answer: Is there a pericardial effusion? Is there cardiac activity?:contentReference[oaicite:30]{index=30}
  • Parasternal long axis (PLAX): Place probe left of the sternum in the 3rd–4th intercostal space; indicator towards patient’s right shoulder. Evaluate left ventricular function and right/left ventricular size.
  • Parasternal short axis (PSAX): Rotate 90° from PLAX; indicator to left shoulder; see left ventricle cross‑section; estimate contractility and look for right ventricular enlargement.
  • Apical four‑chamber (optional): At the apex with indicator towards patient’s left; shows all four chambers.

Key questions

  • Is there a pericardial effusion causing tamponade?:contentReference[oaicite:31]{index=31}
  • Is the heart beating (distinguish PEA from true asystole)?
  • Is the right ventricle dilated compared with the left (suggesting pulmonary embolism)?
  • What is the global left ventricular systolic function?

The RCEM curriculum lists an indicative number of around 10 ELS scans before sign‑off:contentReference[oaicite:32]{index=32}.

Ultrasound‑Guided Vascular Access

Use a high‑frequency linear probe for peripheral venous cannulation when landmarks fail or in patients with difficult access.

Preparation

  • Select a superficial vein (often in the forearm or antecubital fossa). Apply tourniquet to engorge the vein:contentReference[oaicite:33]{index=33}.
  • Use sterile gel or cover the probe with a sterile sheath and apply gel inside the cover.
  • Position the probe so the indicator aligns with your approach (mark the direction on the patient’s arm).

Short‑axis / out‑of‑plane technique

  • Place the probe perpendicular to the vessel; the vessel appears as a circle. Centre it on the screen and adjust depth/gain.
  • Insert the needle at 30–45° angle in the middle of the probe; watch the needle tip as a bright dot entering the vessel:contentReference[oaicite:34]{index=34}.
  • Advance slowly; once the tip is seen in the lumen and flashback occurs, lower the angle and advance the catheter. Confirm tip location in the vessel by ultrasound.

Long‑axis / in‑plane technique

  • Rotate the probe so the vessel appears as a long tube; align the probe with the vessel.
  • Insert the needle parallel to the probe, keeping the entire shaft and tip in view. Watch the tip enter the vessel and thread the catheter.

Practice both approaches; maintain visualisation of the needle tip at all times to avoid posterior wall puncture. RCEM suggests ~10 vascular access scans for sign‑off:contentReference[oaicite:35]{index=35}.

Documentation & Safety

For each scan, record:

  • Indication and patient position.
  • Probe used and exam type (eFAST, AAA, etc.).
  • Views obtained and key findings (e.g., free fluid present/absent, pericardial effusion, right ventricle size).
  • Any limitations (obesity, subcutaneous emphysema, poor windows) and subsequent actions (e.g., CT or formal ultrasound).
  • Store representative still images and/or clips in the patient’s record; label with patient identifiers and date/time.

Safety & Governance

  • Ultrasound is operator‑dependent. Use as an adjunct to clinical assessment; an eFAST negative exam does not exclude injury.
  • Ensure machine cleaning between patients; use appropriate probe covers.
  • Follow your institution’s credentialing process and maintain a log of supervised scans. Competence is judged by quality of scans and reflections, not just numbers:contentReference[oaicite:36]{index=36}.
  • Obtain patient consent where appropriate, and respect privacy when saving images.
